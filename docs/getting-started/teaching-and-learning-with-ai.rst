This page summarises the product idea (or the unique selling points, in marketing parlance)
of OpenBook.

General thoughts on AI in teaching an learning:

- General availability and ubiquity of Large Language Model based AI assistants gave rise to a global debate of modern teaching and learning practises.
- The question is how we teach and learn and if our teaching approaches are still valid in the age of AI.
- Though, anybody long enough in education, will remember, that the discussion is much older, yet much intensified nowadays.
- Educators often felt that previous student generations learnt deeper and achieved more.
- No mater if this actually true, since long educators strive to improve teaching practices, student-centeredness and more.

- What is new is how rapidly AI has changed the field.
- Many teaching and assessment practices, though didactically still useful, seem to be outpaced by recent AI advancements.
- What is the point in writing an essay, when AI can do it in a fraction of time with very little effort, if any at all?
- Yet, we all know, that learning is an active process, that requires effort, involvement, engagement and energy.
- An AI generated summary might be factually correct (with varying quality depending on prompting skill).
- But learners of course betray themselves by taking shortcuts.

- On the other hand it doesn't make sense to forbid AI.
- For one thing, a ban cannot be enforced, anyway.
- And AI will not go away but affect more and more parts of our personal and professional.
- Plus, used right, with the right prompts, human oversight and avoidance of slop, AI can have significant value -- for educators and learners alike.
- So, instead of asking, what teaching and learning practices still make sense in the age of AI we should ask:
- How can we combine the best of both worlds? And how can we support our students learning best?

The project addresses three recurring problems in everyday teaching:

1. Traditional, linear learning material does not fit all learners equally well.
2. Teachers lack transparent feedback about student learning progress.
3. Students lose orientation about priorities, timelines, and prerequisites.

Regarding problem 1:

- Linear material like slide decks, transcripts, scripts or textbooks necessarily embed assumptions about an imaginary student using them.
- They assume certain prior knowledge, motivation, learning goals and skills.
- Good material fits to a large portion of the students using them. But this is very hard to achieve.
- But there is still a chance that for some groups it contains too much information while at the same time for others it contains too little.
- As a result much content, though created with best intents and painstakingly high effort by the educators, is ignored by students.
- A problem that even long before AI was intensified by an ongoing economisation of teaching and learning and "purely goal-oriented" strategies.

Regarding problem 2:

- Learning Management Systems (LMS) like Moodle or ILIAS offer excellent didactical tools for learning and assessment.
- But many educators either are unaware of the possibilities, lack required technical skills are simply cannot use them due to local restrictions.
- At the same time LMS are quite old by today's standards, being in development for 20+ years.
- Things feel dated at places, because the systems acquired lots of functionality over the years and cannot as quickly adopt to new technology or trends.
- More technical savvy educators then use outside tools or even roll their own.
- But then they might lack features like a structured learning model and tracking of learning progress.
- As a result, educators using digital tools (especially in self-study contexts like Inverted Classroom (ICM)) lack data and feedback on students' learning progress.

Regarding problem 3:

- The more materials and information is made available to students, the more they tend to loose orientation.
- Even more so, when the materials and informations is scattered across multiple documents or platforms.
- Students then often claim (e.g. in summative evaluations) that lectures lack a clear thread, even though the thread was arguably given.

OpenBook tries to solve these issues in a unique way.

Solving problem 1:

- First and foremost, it is an LMS itself, built around teaching/learning materials and courses.
- As such and from the even longer history of Computer-Aided Teaching (CAT) tools, it inherits a strong learner model, including learning targets, goals, achievements and more.
- Next, learning materials are structured around a textbook metaphor, due to the long and proven history of textbooks and technical literature to convey knowledge.
- This is important, because while LMS do have interactive activities like quizzes, they are most often used merely to distribute static lecture notes (PDF files).
- OpenBook can do this, too, because when preparing the next lecture tomorrow sometimes a PDF is the most time-saving and straight-forward way to provide extra information to students.
- But student's never learn the PDFs. They learn the textbook pages, that contain the information as well as supporting activities.
- Speaking of which, interactive activities are at the heart of textbooks. Textbooks are not simply "printed text online" but make full use of the web medium.
- Textbooks are not meant to be consumed passively. Interactive activities are used throughout to check learning progress, deepen understanding and more.
- As such some of them are enhanced by the built-in ELISA AI, like activities that assess comprehension, reasoning or practical skills.

Solving problems 2 and 3:

- All these abilities and ELISA AI are used to provide a truly adaptive learning environment.
- Textbooks are linear in nature, since lectures typically also follow a linear structure.
- But the system constantly assesses learning progress and uses AI to determine learner needs.
- As a result, the system guides the learner, chooses from available content and adds extra-content on demand (additional explanations, quizzes, ...)

- Most if not all other existing AI learning assistants and chatbots, are purely output-driven.
- Generating output in response to prompts. No question, no answer. And only answers to what as been explicitly asked.
- OpenBook's ELISA AI is different.
- First, it is not an Add-On. It is at the heart of the application. Many features are built around it.
- The AI acts pro-actively but not in the sense of other modern AI agents, that it is running in background until a certain goal is (hopefully) achieved.
- Instead, it is in constant interaction with the human (humans are always in the loop) and does not pursue goals of its own.
- It's goal instead is to maximize learning outcomes of the learner, for which is assesses prior knowledge, understanding, learning progress and more,
- finds and selects relevant learning materials and activities in the textbooks,
- and suggests, creates and accompanies additional interactive activities like extra quizzes, programming assignments, assessment simulations and more.

- Students never loose orientation, as they are guided by the AI and the system is constantly making visible the learning path, what was achieved and what remains to do.
- Lecturers get insight and feedback via learning dashboards, summarizing learning progress of a group, where students had problems with understanding and more.
- Additionally, the system helps students to prepare questions for class and engage with lecturers and other learners.
- This is rounded by a balance of academic seriousness, playfulness and gamification. Teaching and learning is serious. But is must also be fun to be effective.
